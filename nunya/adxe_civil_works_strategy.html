Section 01

The 10 Biggest Problems in Civil Construction Today

01
Cost Overruns
Projects routinely exceed budgets by 20–80%. Estimates made at bid time rarely survive field conditions.
02
Schedule Delays
Only 25% of large infrastructure projects finish on time. Weather, supply chain, and labor pile on daily.
03
Labor Shortage
The industry is millions of skilled workers short globally. An aging workforce retires faster than new talent arrives.
04
Safety Incidents
Construction has one of the highest fatality rates. One serious incident can shut a project down and cost tens of millions in liability.
05
Supply Chain Fragility
Material prices swing wildly. A steel or concrete shortage in month 6 can derail a project that was on track in month 1.
06
Regulatory Complexity
Permits and compliance requirements multiply every year. One missed filing can halt a project for months.
07
Poor Data & Visibility
Project data lives in spreadsheets and emails. Leadership can't see what's happening until it's too late.
08
Rework & Defects
5–15% of total project cost is wasted fixing work done wrong. Errors caught late cost 10× more to fix.
09
Subcontractor Management
One slow sub cascades into everyone else. Coordinating dozens of independent parties with competing schedules is relentless.
10
Thin Margins & Bad Bids
Civil margins average 2–5%. One underbid project wipes out profits from ten good ones. Estimating is art and science.
Section 02

Inefficiencies by Department

Estimating
  • Manual takeoffsEstimators spend 60–70% of time measuring drawings by hand — slow, error-prone, not scalable.
  • No bid history learningLost bids pile up but are never analyzed. The same pricing mistakes repeat because nobody has time to review.
  • Siloed cost dataActual costs rarely make it back to estimating. Bids are based on outdated assumptions, not today's real costs.
  • Last-minute scramblesProposals assembled in 48-hour rushes. Quality suffers, errors slip through, team burns out.
Field Operations
  • Paper-based reportingDaily logs filled on paper or disconnected spreadsheets. Data is days old by the time it reaches leadership.
  • Equipment downtimeHeavy equipment breaks without warning. Emergency repairs cost $5,000–$25,000/day per machine.
  • Schedule conflictsCrews and equipment clash because scheduling is done in spreadsheets with no conflict detection.
  • Material wasteOver-ordering, theft, and damage add 8–12% to material costs on a typical project.
Engineering & Design
  • Drawing version chaosField teams work from outdated drawings. Revisions take days to reach the crew — sometimes they don't.
  • RFI backlogsRequests pile up. Average resolution is 7–14 days. One slow RFI can halt a crew for days.
  • Design-reality gapsSite conditions rarely match survey data. Problems surface only after ground is broken.
  • Manual QA/QCInspectors walk sites and fill forms. Coverage is partial, documentation inconsistent, defects get missed.
Procurement
  • Reactive buyingMaterials ordered when needed, not before. Rush orders carry 15–30% premiums that quietly kill margins.
  • No supplier performance dataSame vendors used out of habit even when delivery reliability or quality has slipped.
  • Approval bottlenecksA $500 gasket delayed 3 days can stop a million-dollar excavator.
  • Inventory blind spotsNobody knows what's at which yard. Over-purchasing and re-purchasing are common results.
Finance
  • Slow invoicingProgress billing delayed because nobody agrees on what % of work is done. Cash flow suffers.
  • Cost code misallocationHours charged to wrong projects. Job costing reports are unreliable — nobody trusts the numbers.
  • No real-time budget visibilityMonthly reports mean leadership discovers a project is over budget six weeks too late.
  • Change order disputesClients dispute change orders because documentation is weak. The company eats costs it should have recovered.
Safety (HSE)
  • Reactive incident responseIssues discovered after something goes wrong. Near-misses unreported because filing is time-consuming.
  • Inconsistent safety cultureStandards vary wildly between sites and subs. One lax PM creates massive liability.
  • Manual compliance trackingCerts and inspection logs in spreadsheets. Expired credentials are a liability time bomb.
  • PPE compliance gapsWorkers skip gear when unsupervised. Supervisors can't be everywhere.

Section 04 — Primary Domain Intelligence

The Expert's Objections: Voice of the Market

These insights come from a career civil engineer and construction expert. This is not skepticism to overcome — it is the market's actual voice. Every objection below is held by the majority of decision-makers in this industry.

Civil Engineering Expert — Direct Field Intelligence
  • Focus
    Don't try to solve everything at once. A contractor running seven jobs doesn't want a platform — they want relief from one specific, painful problem. Pick one phase and solve it completely. Estimating and cost control are deeply intertwined and the highest-leverage starting point.
  • TCO Risk
    "Go buy seven drones, program them, and hire someone to interpret the data" — that's what an AI drone pitch sounds like to a contractor. The hardware cost, setup complexity, and new headcount required to operate it kills the ROI before the conversation is over. Any solution that requires the contractor to build new infrastructure will lose.
  • Workflow First
    You need to understand how they estimate before you can improve it. "How does MassTech estimate?" is not rhetorical — it's due diligence. The workflow varies significantly between firms and must be understood at ground level before building or selling anything.
  • Optimization > Revolution
    Contractors are not looking for transformation. They are looking for optimization of what they already do well. The pitch is not "do it differently" — it is "do what you already do, but faster, with fewer errors, and with better information."
  • Trust Deficit
    Experienced contractors have seen every software pitch for decades and watched most of them fail in the field. Skepticism is the default and it is earned. Credibility comes from demonstrating that you understand their actual workflow — not from showing a demo.
"I'm not sold on the whole thing. There are certain items that can be optimized — and that's what knowing what they're doing and how they're doing it."

Section 06

Go-To-Market Strategy

The Wedge: Enter Through Estimating

Estimating is where money is won or lost before a shovel hits the ground. It is the most manual, most hated, highest-stakes phase of the project lifecycle. The estimator is usually the sharpest person at the firm — doing work that is beneath their actual intellectual value. That tension is the opening.

The Pitch That Converts (Not Just "That's Cool")
"Right now, you spend 60% of your time measuring and calculating. What if you spent that 60% on the 10% of the estimate that actually determines whether you win or lose — the risk assessment, the strategy, the judgment calls? That's all you, forever. We eliminate the grunt work so you can focus on the part nobody else can do."

Execution Steps

  1. Leverage the Expert as a Living Reference

    Your father is not just an advisor — he is the proof of concept. If ADXE can earn his genuine endorsement, that story sells itself: "This skeptic with 40 years in the field said this is the first thing that actually makes sense." Build the product around his specific objections first.

  2. Do Workflow Discovery at Two Target Firms

    Identify two mid-size contractors (the MassTech comparison is the right scale). Spend a day with their estimating team — not a demo, just observation. Answer three questions: What software are they using? Where does the estimate break down most? What does a bad week look like personally?

  3. Build the Minimum Credible Product Around One Workflow

    Based on discovery, identify the single step in the estimating workflow that is most painful and most automatable. Build a focused solution for that step only. Resist the urge to build the whole platform. One thing done remarkably well opens every door.

  4. Pilot on a Real Project with Real Numbers

    Offer the first engagement at low or no cost in exchange for documented results. Define success metrics before the pilot starts. Specific, measurable outcomes — time saved, errors caught, dollars recovered — become the case study that drives every subsequent sale.

  5. Build Integrations to Dominant Platforms Before Scaling

    Procore, Sage, Viewpoint, and Trimble are already in most firms. ADXE's solution must plug into these seamlessly. "Works with what you have" removes the biggest friction point in procurement and lets champions sell it internally without fighting their IT team.

  6. Expand from Estimating → Cost Control → Full Project Intelligence

    Once estimating is owned, cost control is the natural expansion — your father identified these as intertwined. A contractor who trusts ADXE's estimating intelligence will want that intelligence to follow the project through execution. That is the upsell path with zero additional acquisition cost.
